Business Ethics In The Face Of Monopolistic Threats

In view of the increasing commercialization of the Free Software movement, it has become important to clarify a matter of business ethics that is all too often overlooked.

This has to do with monopolies. Copyright law allows the authors of computer programs to have, for a limited period of time, monopoly rights to modifying and copying the program. For the sake of illustration, let's compare this monopoly to a monopoly of oranges: Imagine that in the whole world there was only one place where orange trees grow, and imagine furthermore that I am the owner of that area. Then I have a monopoly of oranges. Let's suppose that I am generous and put up a sign that says "Everyone is welcome to come and pick oranges". It won't take long before some entrepreneurial people pick more oranges than they want to eat themselves, and start a business of selling oranges. As long as there are still plenty of oranges for everyone who wants to pick oranges, I wouldn't mind that. Effectively, what the businesses are selling is the convenience of being able to conveniently buy the oranges, without bothering about the harvest and transportation of the oranges.

This situation is comparable to a market for software which can be freely copied and redistributed, without any monopolies.

However, I would get very upset if someone came and harvested all oranges in order to gain control of the market and sell the oranges at a higher price. That would be exploiting my generosity in a totally unethical manner.

In the same way, when I contribute to a Free Software project, then I my contribution to be for everyone's benefit, and I do not want anyone to turn it into a monopoly. In fact the whole point of the Free Software movement is to get rid of software monopolies. (Proprietary software is software which has an "owner" who has a monopoly through having the right to decide who can see the source code, who may modify the program, and who may copy it for purposes of further distribution. Free Software is software where every user has these rights.)

The Free Software community welcomes businesses to get involved. We don't mind when the involved companies derive a profit from their involvement (why else would be profit-oriented company get involved) as long as these companies fight together with us at least against the worst software monopolies.

In the context of DotGNU project this means:

  1. If you earn money through distributing an improved version of some DotGNU software, we expect you to also contribute the improvements back to the development project, for everyone's benefit. While the GNU General Public License does not require you to do this, we will feel that you have exploited our generosity in an unethical manner if you don't do this.
  2. With .NET, Microsoft has introduced some technologies (in particular C#, IL, and a significant amount of class libraries) that present a dilemma for the GNU project and for the Free Software movement in general, and also for the Open Source movement: On one hand, if these technologies are not available on our operating systems, then we must fear that these Free/Open operating systems would die the slow death of software that is not updated to adapt it to the evolving needs of the users. On the other hand, endorsing these technologies, the specifications of which are effectively controlled by Microsoft, Inc., has significant risks also. There are strong indications that from a business strategy perspective, these technologies were designed specifically with the goal to unleash a destructive Hailstorm against the Open Source movement which has become a serious threat for Microsoft's monopolistic strategies. When a Free Software or Open Source company generates profits by simply playing along with Microsoft's plan, that is therefore like a betrayal against the Free Software and Open Source community. (On the other hand, there is nothing wrong with earning money through DotGNU'ish efforts to counter the threat of the .NET in which Microsoft Inc. wants to catch us.)
  3. The DotGNU project holds that with .NET-related stuff, it is not acceptable for a company to ask contributors to assign copyright so that the company "can use a dual license like Mozilla does for commercial purposes." The Mozilla project is a community effort to contribute to Free Software which competes against "Internet Explorer", a program that was (in the judgment of US courts of law) promoted by means of an "illegal monopoly" . For .NET-related stuff, when a company aligns its commercial interests with those of Microsoft, then that company has no moral justification for asking volunteer contributors to similarly assign copyright.
